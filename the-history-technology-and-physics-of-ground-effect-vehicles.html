Physics

The ground-effect is a phenomenon which occurs when an aircraft is flying close to the ground. To the pilot, it may feel as if the aircraft is ‘floating’ above the ground on a cushion of air[1]. It can occur during take-off, or landing, and even affects helicopters.

Reasons

The Ground Effect occurs because, as an aircraft approaches the ground, the vortices of air that had formed around the tips of the wings are disrupted, as the vortexes hit the ground, and so are forced to be smaller [2].

As the wing-tip-vortices get smaller, they lead to less downwash. After the air going over the wing rolls off of the back of the wing, it changes direction, so that it goes downwards[3] . This means that some of the ‘relative wind’ (the direction of the wind closest to the plane, near to the wings) points downwards, it is opposite to the direction that the plane is moving (e.g if a plane is moving forwards, the relative wind will be moving backwards, relative to the plane).

This also known as the ‘oncoming airmass’.

The force of lift always acts perpendicular to the relative wind[2]  [4].

Why this causes the ground-effect:

  • Usually, when an aircraft is in the air, the downwash leads to the relative wind pointing downwards, due to the deflection of the wind.
  • lift acts perpendicular to the direction of relative wind.
  • This leads to lift acting on the aircraft, but a slight amount backwards.
    • This effect is called ‘induced drag’.
  • When the plane’s wings are closer to the ground, downwash is smaller. The lift that acts backwards will be smaller, and so closer to purely vertical.

This will lead to the plane experiencing ‘less drag’, even though it is just experiencing the same amount of lift, just acting more vertically.


This leads to the feeling of less drag while landing or taking off. The intensity of the ground-effect relies on the wing’s distance from the ground, and its length. The wing’s length is important, as, the longer the wing, the more downwash will be produced, and the greater the reduction in induced drag will be[5] .

Pilots need to be able to understand, and account for the ground-effect when landing and taking off. To do this, they need to know when the ground-effect begins to take effect. The ground effect takes place when the aircraft is within its wingspan-length above the ground. The closer you get to the ground, the more compressed the wing-tip-vortices become, and the aircraft experiences less induced drag.

Due to this, aircraft with lower wings experience the ground-effect to a higher extent.

This effect can be harnessed, and combined with a few other technologies to enable vehicles specifically designed to use the ground-effect to be able to travel efficiently.

During World War II, While flying long distances between islands in the Pacific, American B-29s sometimes experienced engine failures. To get back to where they would be safe, the crews often used the  ground-effect to conserve the fuel they had left, and increase their range[6] . As well as this, the Dornier DO-X (a large seaplane), when making its (only) transatlantic flight in 1930, flew only a few metres above the sea almost all the way, to conserve fuel[5] [7]

Dangers:

Though the ground-effect may be useful at certain times, it can also have some drawbacks which a pilot may need to take into consideration when taking off, or landing.

Pilots in traditional planes may overshoot the runway if not accounting for the ground effect, during take-off. On the 26th of October 1952, a De Havilland Comet could not take off, overran the runway, and had to terminate the landing[8] [9] . This happened because, when planes are in the ground-effect, the angle at which stalling happens is smaller (by 2 to 4 º), so planes can easily rotate too much while taking off, and stall[10] .

 

Design

Ground effect vehicles have a couple of advantages over both normal aircraft, and land or sea vehicles (as well as some disadvantages).

The major advantages include[6] :

  • They are much more Efficient than traditional aircraft, due to the reduced induced drag they experience, allowing them to travel farther on the same amount of fuel.
  • They are much faster than land vehicles or boats, and so are able to be much more practical in a variety of situations.

To effectively harness the ground-effect, vehicles must incorporate technologies which enable them to use the ground-effect as much as possible.

Types of GEV

There are three main types of ground-effect vehicles[11] :

  • Type A: An aircraft that is certified only to work when in the ground-effect.
  • Type B: An aircraft that is certified to work in the ground-effect, and also temporarily fly to 150 Metres or under.
  • Type C: An aircraft that is able to do the same as [Type B], but is also able to fly above 150 metres for short amounts of time, in an emergency, or to avoid obstacles.

Ground-effect vehicles are regulated, and certified by the International Maritime Organisation, as most of them fly over only water. This is because open water tends to be relatively smooth (the ground-effect works best over a smooth surface) and have few, or easily seen and avoidable obstacles. Due to this, they fall under the IMO’s regulation.

Wing Shapes

Ground-effect vehicles usually have one of three wing shapes, each of which is useful in different situations.

The first of these is the earliest, and most simplistic – the straight-wing design. This design lends itself well to larger GEVs, as it is made up of two wings, like a normal plane. However, these wings are usually short and broad. GEVs with the straight-wing design are not very stable, and need a large tail to act as a stabiliser. Aircraft using this design tend to be vertically stable[12] . This design is notable, as it was used in the design of the Ekranoplane.

GEVs can also have ‘Tandem’ wings. These are a pair of wings, and can be arranged in a variety of positions around the body of the aircraft. Most modern Tandem-wing GEVs have one pair of wings at the front, and one pair of wings at the back. This configuration allows for higher (longitudinal) stability [12], and so is used much more in lightweight, smaller GEVs – the type of aircraft that are being developed a lot more in the modern day. This design is notable because it was used by Günther Jörg in his tandem-airfoil Flairboat [13] .

The last type of wing configuration is the reverse delta wing. This design also employs a large tail for stability, the wings are also often very broad. This design is very stable, and also allows flight for short distances at a higher altitude, making it useful for type B GEVs[14] . It is notable, as it was designed and implemented by Messerschmitt Me 163 Komet inventor Alexander Lippisch in his GEVs

Advantages and Disadvantages of GEVs

Advantages:

Disadvantages:

·         They are much more efficient, as they do not have to contend with as much induced drag, so can go much farther on the same amount of fuel.

·         Due to the fact that stay outside of the water, they also encounter less water-resistance, and so can be much faster than boats.

·         Underwater Torpedoes and mines can be avoided, and the small distance from the water also makes them virtually undetectable using RADAR and SONAR.

·         If the GEV breaks down or malfunctions, it would be a lot less dangerous than if the same happened to a plane in flight.

·         They can be quite unstable, so can be hard to fly.

·         They need a generally smooth, flat surface to travel over, so waves are a problem. The larger the GEV, the higher it can fly over the surface, yet larger GEVs are harder to control, and produce.

·         It tends to be hard to avoid any large obstacles.

·         GEVs tend to be loud and have high maintenance costs.

 

History

The ground-effect had been observed by pilots since the beginning of flight, originally incorrectly being attributed to a mysterious magnetic force. However, the ground effect was not well understood, and so many people investigated it to try and see how it worked. In 1934, the USA national advisory committee for aeronautics translated French research, ‘Ground Effect on the Take-off and Landing of Airplanes ’[15] .  In it, the author Maurice le Sueur suggests that the effect could be used as a, ‘rapid and at the same time economic form of locomotion’[16, p. 24/25] . After this report was published, the awareness and possibilities of the ground-effect became much more widespread, though it was still restricted to select aeronautical circles.

The Ekranoplanes

The Soviet Union was always looking for new innovations that could help them prove that they were superior than the West, Nikita Khrushchev was the leader of the Soviet Union from 1953 to 1964, and was especially drawn to innovative and novel projects and ideas [17] .

Rostislav Alexeyev was the director and chief designer of the Central Hydrofoil Bureau, he had designed boats using hydrofoils for the Soviet Union previously, such as the Raketa[18] . He had had an idea to lift a boat’s hydrofoil out of the water, enabling it to use the ground-effect to fly above the water, quickly, yet efficiently. He began testing the concept out with small scale models of his design, but needed more funding to make his designs in real life.

He called these designs ‘Ekranoplanes’, or ‘screen planes’, after the Russian name for the ground-effect – the screen effect.

Alexeyev managed to secure the funding by showing that his ekranoplanes could be useful to the Military. They easily avoided mines, as they flew above the water, were much more efficient, and could carry higher loads than aeroplanes, and could also go much faster than ships. Crucially, Ekranoplanes could avoid both SONAR, and RADAR, as they flew too low to be detected by RADAR, and completely out of the water, so, to SONAR, they were invisible.

To the USSR’s military officials, the ground-effect was the future.

After receiving the funding, Alexeyev and his team Made Small prototypes to dip their toes into building Ekranoplanes, and prove that their ideas were feasible. On the 22nd July 1961, Alexeyev and his team set their first prototype, the ‘samokhodnaya model-1’[19] - or ‘self-propelled model’ - onto the Caspian sea, and started their first test-flight.

Using the lessons learnt from the SM-1, the team built the SM-2. It would be their most important prototype yet. It was completed in March 1962, and demonstrated to Nikita Krushchev, who was excited by the idea, and gave them a Much higher budget, to build much larger Ekranoplanes[20].

After this, they built More prototypes, until the SM-4. The Soviet Navy evaluated this design, and decided that it showed a good understanding of Ekranoplane design. They then ordered a much larger, experimental Ekranoplane.

The team made a quarter-scale prototype, called the SM-5, and it proved that the design would work[20] .

Alexeyev and his team had finished building it by 1966.

The full-scale prototype was built in the Krasnoye Sormovo Shipyard in Gorky, made by a team led by Alexeyev and the famous shipwright V. Efimov. It was hauled onto the river Volga on the 22nd June 1966, and transported to Kaspiysk Naval base, on the Caspian Sea. The entire operation was done at night, to keep the prototype a secret.[21]

This was called the Korabl Maket (or KM) which translated to ‘ship prototype’. It was a class A GEV. It was the largest aircraft in the world at the time[21] and is the second largest aircraft ever built. It had a wingspan of 37.6m, was 92m long, it had a maximum take-off weight of 544 Tonnes and would fly 5-10m over the water. It was tested in the Caspian Sea, and the Soviet Union was very secretive about the entire project, assigning it to the Soviet navy as a boat, though test pilots from the Air force would pilot it.

The KM was tested for 15 years, while Alexeyev worked on other Ekranoplane projects until, in 1980. It crashed following an error made by the pilot, no attempts were made to try to recover it[22] . By this time, the Soviet Union had a new leader, Leonid Brezhnev, who was much more sceptical of unproven concepts[17]  and who decided to stop the Ekranoplane project. However, he let a few projects, already nearing their ends, finish.

This project was the A-90 Orlyonok (meaning eaglet). This was a much smaller Ekranoplane, weighing 140 tonnes and measuring 58 metres long, and was a Class B/C GEV, having one turbroprop engine, and two turbojet engines, with which it could travel at up to 400km/h. It could fly at altitudes much higher, but normally flew 200-400m above the surface of the water [23] It was capable of crossing the Caspian sea, carrying roughly 100 troops, which would be its main function[23]. Five Orlyonoks were made but only four entered service in 1979, In 1993, they were taken out of service with only three remaining [23] . It was the most successful Ekranoplane.

The Plans for the KM were also later used to make an even larger Ekranoplane - the Lun-class Ekranoplane. Lun means ‘harrier’, and It was designed to be used in war, with six Mosquito guided missiles attached to its back, and be just as fast as the KM.

It was built, and entered ‘service’ with the navy in 1987, but sat unused in the base, and later at the bottom of the Caspian Sea, until 2020, after which there are plans to move it into a museum.

Alexeyev was injured on January 14th 1980 while testing an Ekranoplane to be shown off at the 1980 Moscow Olympics[24] . He died three days later, having been demoted from director and chief designer of the Central Hydrofoil Bureau to a Government employee[17] .

 

The Bartini Beriev VVA-14

Robert Bartini was a Hungarian-born Aircraft designer. He was illegitimately born to a baron of Austro-Hungarian Nobility, and given to a peasant family when his mother drowned herself. He moved to Italy, and then the Soviet Union after Fascism rose in italy in 1922 [25] . He helped design aircraft almost immediately, working with Andrei Tupolev designing the TU-2. A plane he designed; the Bartini Stal-7 broke the international speed record in 1936. In the 1950s, He grew increasingly interested in GEVs, and developed a small GEV of his own, the Beriev Be-1, though it was seen as not useful, and only one was ever made[26] .

Bartini then began on one of the most ambitious aircraft projects in history. The Soviet Union needed an aircraft that could be an anti-submarine aircraft, to combat the USA’s Polaris Missile submarines. Bartini developed a concept that could take off and land vertically, use the ground effect, and land in water, to completely fulfil the requirements needed[27] . The Soviet Military liked the idea, and began supporting

The aircraft was named the Bartini Beriev VVA-14 (for vertical take-off amphibious aircraft ), and research was begun.

Three prototypes were to be built: one just to see how well it could fly as well as its inflatable pontoons, the other to test out the 12 lift-jets and the last with the anti-submarine weapons.

However, the pontoons ended up being much harder to get to deploy properly, and so temporary metal ballasts were fitted instead[27] . The first prototype was the only one ever built, and was capable of flying, with it being able to stay 8m above the sea when in ground-effect .

However, one of the main aspects of the entire design – the lift jets that would enable VTOL – could not be produced, as the engine company that was given the task of making them was both overburdened with other projects, and could not make any engines powerful enough for the VVA-14.

 Bartini had hopes for a flying aircraft-carrier, but, due to the lack of promise in the VVA-14 project, as well as other GEV projects, it was never considered. [27]

Bartini Died in 1974, and the VVA-14 project was scrapped. The only Prototype was moved to The Central Air-Force Museum in 1987. Bartini was thought to be too ahead of his time, one of his workers called him an ‘alien’[28]. The VVA-14 is now in disrepair.

Modern GEVs

There are many modern GEVs in development, one of these is the Chaika A-050, a small Russian GEV. Though the plans have not yet been revealed, it is known that it will have a take-off weight of 54 tonnes and will be powered by R-195 engines, being able to go 250-300 mph for 3000 km[29] .

Australia, for a short while in the mid-2000s developed a GEV called the ‘sea-eagle’, but was only used on trade routes[30] .

The Airfish 8 is one of the most promising GEVs that is being actively developed. It is a class A, reverse-delta wing GEV, and is going to mainly be used for ‘logistical, transport and marine patrol operations’. It has been developed since 2004, and has been registered for use in Malaysia. It is made of carbon-fibre reinforced plastic to make it lightweight. It has a carrying capacity of 360kg and six or eight seats. It is powered by two general-motors LS3 V8 engines[31] .

The SeaFalcon SF-08 is a German Class A Reverse-delta wing GEV project which is likely not to happen, but still had a few prototypes that looked promising. It was designed to allow people to easily travel between the many islands of the Caribbean. It can fly at 80-100 knots, and an Indonesian Firm now owns 6 of them. The company was later bought by Wigetworks, the same company that created the Airfish 8. [32] [33]

Iran seems to be very interested in the idea of using GEVS for military purposes. In 2010, it unveiled its HESA Bavar 2s, small Class-A Reverse-delta wing GEVS. They are small, so that they can evade Radar more effectively. By 2015, Iran supposedly had 3 squadrons of Bavar 2s in service, using them mainly for scouting purposes. They can travel at up to 115 mph and have night vision (essentially infra-red) cameras mounted on them. In 2015, satellite images showed what may be a larger GEV in production in an Irani Shipyard[34] . They also have machine-guns, and supposedly small rockets. [35] [36].

Conclusion

Ground-Effect Vehicles are an immensely interesting Type of aircraft. They have innumerable uses, and use fascinating physics to be able to work. They are entwined in intriguing history, and are constantly being re-designed and re-invented. As well as that, the idea of flying just above the water on a layer of air, more efficiently, whether a tiny scale model, or a massive Ekranoplane will always be an enchanting idea.
