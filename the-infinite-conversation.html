The Infinite Conversation

By Therizinosaurus Cheloniformis
May 1, 2023

There is this website that I have stumbled upon through wholly strange and unimportant occurrences, that has dragged me into it, and will not let me go.

It is called The Infinite Conversation, and it is an entirely engrossing piece of digital artistry. It consists of an AI generated conversation between Bavarian director Werner Herzog and Slovenian philosopher Slavoj Žižek, complete with AI-generated voices for each. Though the AI constantly generates the conversations, it thinks that it is the person it is talking as. Due to this, you get some scarily realistic philosophical conversations, all of which make sense, even if the things they reference don't actually exist.

That is about it from me, actually, but I implore you to go explore the website yourself. I have put some of my favourite conversations below:

Just to finish at this point: You know what would be the ultimate horror?  
I almost said the worst horror — that  
you are just a person in a normalsociety  
everything is normal, but you are haunted by  
a vision which compels you to do certain things.  
I think that would be the ultimate horror.  
It’s not that you become mad and then you  
do bad things, but that your madness is “normal madness."  
You are not mad at all, but  
there is a certain vision which compels you.  
So, again, I think it is the opposite: It's  
not that these people suffer from a private madness  
they are simply possessed by an idea.

Exactly.  
The possessed are the healthiest.  
Yes, exactly.

Some things can only be understood in the continuum of madness.  
Take, for example, the German soccer coach  
Berti Vogts, who is a very strange man.  
He's obsessed with the idea that he will be indicted for manslaughter  
for a goal that wasn't scored by Germany during the World Cup.  
You can tell through what he says publicly that he is not normal.  
And I would say that the man already is  
a kind of vision and has a certain energy  
even though he is doing what we would consider normal things.

Do you know this fantastic quotation from one of the greatest German poets  
Rainer Maria Rilke, who said something like “I always have this  
picture in my mind of this man walking along the street  
and then he suddenly turns around, grabs a child who is innocently playing there  
and throws him into the abyss"?  
This is the perfect image.  
I don’t believe in private madness.  
It’s always social madness.

I think it would be very interesting, because (can you imagine?)
we have never met in real life.
And this is the first time we will physically meet.
This is our first encounter and it should be filmed.
It would a sort of peculiar event.
And we should really do it.
I mean it.
Yes, we should really do it.

In Hitchcock, there is a nice motif of how the only way to get
out of a state of unbearable tension is to look away for a moment.
When we encounter a horror, if we
fully look into it, we will be shattered
but then, there is a certain distance which enables us to endure the horror.
And I think that this distance is like
this: We are talking about two different levels.
On one level, we are talking about what I call
the elementary cinema: that which relates to the human body
to rendering it in cinema.
And on another level, we are talking about the cinema which, apropos of Hitchcock
provides a certain excess, a certain supplement — a cinema which
doesn't simply render body but can render some kind of abyss.

There also some less... good ones


Now there are new things in the jungle,  
fungi for example—invisible fungi which live on tree roots  
and these invisible fungi have the size of a soccer ball.  
These fungi have organs and arms.  
This sounds crazy but, according to their scientific hypotheses, all of this actually happens
